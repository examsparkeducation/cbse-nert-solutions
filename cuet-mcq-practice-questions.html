Understanding the CUET MCQ Exam Pattern

Before you blindly start solving questions, you must understand the battlefield. The CUET exam is entirely objective, meaning every single question is a Multiple Choice Question (MCQ). The paper is broadly divided into three main sections:

  • Section IA & IB (Languages): Tests your reading comprehension, vocabulary, and grammar. You have to attempt 40 out of 50 questions.
  • Section II (Domain-Specific Subjects): This is where your Class 12 NCERT knowledge is tested. Whether you chose Physics, Accountancy, or History, you must attempt 40 out of 50 questions.
  • Section III (General Test): Designed for specific vocational or open-eligibility courses. It tests General Knowledge, Current Affairs, General Mental Ability, and Quantitative Reasoning. You have to attempt 50 out of 60 questions.

The Danger of Negative Marking in CUET

Unlike your school board exams where attempting every question is highly encouraged, CUET punishes guesswork. The marking scheme awards +5 marks for every correct answer and deducts -1 mark for every incorrect answer. Unattempted questions yield 0 marks.

This is exactly why practicing a high volume of CUET MCQ practice questions is critical. Daily practice trains your brain to differentiate between a "calculated guess" and a "blind guess." Through consistent testing, you learn the art of skipping questions that are designed purely to eat up your time and trigger negative marking.

Domain-Wise MCQ Strategy for CUET 2026

Your preparation strategy must adapt to the specific nature of your chosen subjects. Here is a breakdown of how to approach different streams:

1. Science Domain (Physics, Chemistry, Biology, Maths)

For the Science stream, CUET MCQs are a mix of direct NCERT lines and JEE/NEET-level foundational numericals. In Physics and Physical Chemistry, speed is your biggest hurdle. Generate practice sets focused purely on numerical-based MCQs to improve your calculation speed. In Biology and Inorganic Chemistry, focus heavily on Assertion-Reasoning (A-R) questions and exceptions to general rules.

2. Commerce Domain (Accountancy, Business Studies, Economics)

Commerce papers in CUET test your conceptual depth. For Accountancy, practice MCQs that require you to calculate ratios or partnership goodwill mentally, without drawing massive ledger accounts. In Business Studies, expect a barrage of Case-Study MCQs where a business scenario is given, and you must identify the principle of management being applied.

3. Humanities Domain (History, Pol Science, Geography)

Humanities students must focus on chronological order and fact-matching. The CUET paper heavily features "Match the Following" and "Arrange in Chronological Order" MCQs. You must practice questions that force you to remember exact dates of treaties, sequences of historical events, and specific locations on maps.

4. The General Test (GT) Strategy

The GT section requires a completely different approach because it is not restricted to your Class 12 syllabus. You need a daily dose of Logical Reasoning and Quantitative Aptitude MCQs. Practice number series, blood relations, direction sense, and basic Class 8-level mathematics (percentages, profit/loss, time/work).

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. Is the Class 12 NCERT syllabus enough for CUET domain subjects?

Yes, the National Testing Agency (NTA) has clearly specified that the syllabus for Section II (Domain Subjects) is strictly based on the Class 12 NCERT curriculum. However, the difficulty level and analytical depth of the MCQs are much higher than standard board exams.

2. How many questions do I have to attempt in CUET?

CUET provides internal choices to help students. For Languages and Domain subjects, you typically have to attempt 40 out of 50 questions. For the General Test, you must attempt 50 out of 60 questions. Choosing which questions to skip is a vital strategy.
