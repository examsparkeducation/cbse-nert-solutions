What are Case Study Based Questions?

In the Class 10 Science board exam, Section E typically contains 3 Case Study (or Passage-Based) questions, usually carrying 4 marks each (totaling 12 marks). The format is very specific:

  • You are provided with a "Case", which could be a paragraph describing a real-world scenario, a scientific experiment, a statistical table, or a complex diagram.
  • This case is followed by 3 to 4 sub-questions. Generally, there are two short questions of 1 mark each and one slightly detailed question of 2 marks (which often has an internal choice).

The biggest misconception among students is treating this like an English Reading Comprehension passage. Unlike an English passage, the direct answers are rarely written inside the text. The passage merely sets the context; you must use your own NCERT scientific knowledge to answer the sub-questions.

The Proven 3-Step Hack to Solve Any Case Study

Students waste precious exam time reading a 200-word paragraph multiple times, only to realize the questions have very little to do with the exact wording of the text. Here is the secret 3-step framework to maximize your speed and accuracy:

Step 1: The Reverse Engineering Hack (Read the Questions First)
Do not start by reading the passage. Jump straight to the sub-questions. Why? Because identifying what the examiner is asking immediately tells your brain which chapter and concept the case study belongs to. Often, you can answer the 1-mark sub-questions without even reading the passage!

Step 2: Skim and Highlight the Variables
Now, go back and read the passage quickly. You are not reading for a story; you are hunting for variables, chemical formulas, values (like voltage or resistance), or specific terminology. If the passage mentions "a solution turning red litmus blue," immediately write "BASE" next to it with your pencil.

Step 3: Connect to the Core NCERT Concept
Every case study is a textbook concept wearing a disguise. If the passage is about "a person struggling to read the newspaper at the breakfast table," the core concept is the Hypermetropia defect from the Human Eye chapter. Formulate your answer using standard scientific keywords from that specific NCERT chapter.

Chapter-Wise Important Topics for Case Studies

Examiners cannot make a case study out of thin air. They rely on specific, application-heavy chapters. If you are searching for case study questions class 10 science, ensure you practice the following high-probability topics:

1. Biology: Life Processes and Heredity

Biology is the most fertile ground for case studies. In Life Processes, expect a passage detailing the procedure of Hemodialysis (Artificial Kidney) or a table showing the composition of urine to test your knowledge of excretion. The double circulation of the human heart, represented via block diagrams, is another favorite.

In Heredity, you will almost certainly encounter a case study based on Mendel's Pea Plant experiments. You might be given a scenario about crossing tall and short plants and asked to calculate the phenotypic ratio of the F2 generation.

2. Chemistry: Acids, Bases and Carbon Compounds

In Chemistry, case studies are highly experimental. For Acids, Bases, and Salts, practice scenarios based on "pH in everyday life"—such as tooth decay, bee stings, or soil acidity. Passages describing the Chlor-alkali process (preparation of Sodium Hydroxide) along with its byproducts are extremely common.

In Carbon and its Compounds, expect a case study on Saponification (soap making) or the cleansing action of soaps and detergents, often accompanied by a diagram of a micelle.

3. Physics: Light and Electricity

Physics case studies will test your numerical ability and diagrammatic understanding. In the Electricity chapter, you might get a paragraph about the wiring of a household (Domestic Electric Circuits). The questions will ask you to calculate the total power consumed, the cost of electricity, or why appliances are connected in parallel.

For Light - Reflection and Refraction, practice case studies involving an experimental setup of a student placing a candle in front of a concave mirror at various distances. You must know how the nature of the image changes based on the object's position.
